Section A — Establish the Context
The story of LibreOffice begins with the development of OpenOffice.org, an open-source office suite that was created as an alternative to proprietary software such as Microsoft Office. In the late 1990s, Microsoft Office dominated the desktop software market, and its file formats were controlled by Microsoft. This made it difficult for individuals, educational institutions, and governments to use alternative software, because documents created in Microsoft Office did not always work correctly in other programs. When Sun Microsystems acquired Star Division in 1999, it decided to release the StarOffice code as open source under the name OpenOffice.org. This decision allowed developers from around the world to contribute to the project and helped create a free office suite that anyone could use, modify, and distribute.
Although OpenOffice.org was open source in terms of licensing, the governance of the project was not completely open. Sun Microsystems required contributors to sign a copyright assignment agreement before their code could be included in the project. This meant that even though volunteers wrote a large portion of the software, Sun still held legal rights over the code. In addition to this, Sun employees controlled the release schedule, decided which features would be included, and had the final authority over the direction of the project. As a result, the community could contribute ideas and improvements, but they did not have equal power in decision-making.
This governance structure created a situation where the project was legally open source but practically controlled by a single company. Many contributors accepted this arrangement at the time because Sun was generally seen as supportive of open source software. However, the structure itself made the project vulnerable to problems in the future. Since the company owned the copyright and controlled the project, any change in corporate leadership could affect the entire community.
This is the structural feature that made the fork possible and eventually unavoidable. Because the code was released under an open source license, the community had the legal right to copy the code and create a new project. At the same time, because the governance was centralized under Sun, the community did not have the power to change the way the project was managed. This combination meant that if trust between the company and the community was ever broken, the only real option would be to fork the project and start a new one.
The situation changed dramatically in 2010 when Oracle Corporation acquired Sun Microsystems. Until that point, many contributors were willing to tolerate the governance problems in OpenOffice.org because Sun was seen as a relatively friendly steward of open source projects. Sun had a history of supporting developer communities, and even though it held strong control over OpenOffice.org, contributors believed that the company’s intentions were not harmful. However, the acquisition by Oracle created uncertainty and fear within the community. Oracle was known primarily as a commercial enterprise software company, and it had a reputation for aggressively protecting its intellectual property. This caused many developers to worry that Oracle would treat OpenOffice.org as a business asset instead of a community project.
After the acquisition, Oracle did not immediately change the license of OpenOffice.org, but several of its decisions reduced the trust that contributors had in the company. The copyright assignment requirement remained in place, meaning that developers still had to give joint ownership of their contributions to Oracle. This felt more dangerous under Oracle than it had under Sun, because Oracle had already shown that it was willing to use legal action to defend its interests in other projects. Oracle also introduced a commercial product based on OpenOffice.org, which made it clear that the company intended to use the community’s work to generate revenue. In addition, communication between Oracle and the developer community became limited, and important decisions were made without consulting contributors.
The acquisition acted as a trigger even though the copyright assignment problem had existed for many years. The reason for this is that the problem was not only legal but also based on trust. Under Sun, contributors believed that the company would not misuse its control, so they were willing to continue working within the existing system. When Oracle took over, that trust disappeared. The same governance structure that had been tolerated before now felt risky, because the community no longer believed that the company shared their goals. Once trust was lost, the centralized control of the project became unacceptable to many contributors.
This story shows the difference between legal openness and practical openness in a software project. Legally, OpenOffice.org was open source because anyone could view the code, modify it, and distribute it according to the license. However, in practice, the community did not have real control over the project’s direction. Decisions about releases, features, and long-term goals were made by the company that owned the copyright. This meant that the project was open in terms of code, but not fully open in terms of governance. When the community realized that legal freedom alone was not enough to guarantee independence, the idea of creating a fork became more realistic.
As the concerns about Oracle’s control increased, discussions began inside the OpenOffice.org community about what options were available. Because the software was open source, the developers had the legal freedom to fork the project and continue development independently. However, forking was not a simple decision. OpenOffice.org had millions of users, a large codebase, and years of work behind it. Creating a new project would mean dividing the community, confusing users, and rebuilding infrastructure such as websites, documentation, and release systems. Even though the license allowed a fork, the cost of doing so was very high, which is why the community had not taken this step earlier when the same governance problems existed under Sun.
The situation finally reached a breaking point when it became clear that Oracle was not willing to change the way the project was governed. Many contributors believed that the only way to protect the future of the software was to create a new organization that would not be controlled by a single company. In September 2010, leading members of the OpenOffice.org community announced the creation of The Document Foundation and started a new project called LibreOffice. The new foundation was designed to be independent and non-profit, with rules that prevented any single corporation from having complete control. Contributors would keep the copyright to their own work, and decisions would be made by elected members of the community instead of corporate management.
The fork happened not because the code was closed, but because the governance was closed. This is the key lesson from the situation that led to LibreOffice. Open source licenses can guarantee the freedom to use and modify code, but they cannot guarantee that a project will be managed in a fair or transparent way. When a single company controls the copyright, the release process, and the decision-making authority, the community may feel that their work is not truly their own. In such a situation, the only way to regain control is to fork the project, even if that decision creates short-term difficulties.
Therefore, the LibreOffice fork was not a sudden reaction but the result of a structural problem that existed for many years. The centralized governance of OpenOffice.org made the fork possible, while the Oracle acquisition made it unavoidable by breaking the trust that had kept the community together. This event demonstrates that true openness in software projects requires not only open licenses but also open governance, shared ownership, and mutual trust between companies and contributors.

Section B — The Ethics of Corporate Control
The conflict that led to the creation of LibreOffice raises an important ethical question about the relationship between corporations and open source communities. When a company invests money, infrastructure, and employees into building an open source project, it may feel justified in wanting to control the direction of that project. At the same time, open source development depends heavily on volunteers who contribute their time and skills without direct payment. This creates a tension between corporate ownership and community participation. The case of OpenOffice.org shows how this tension can become a serious problem when the balance between the two sides is not clearly defined.
One argument in favor of corporate control is that companies provide resources that communities alone often cannot. Large open source projects require servers, legal support, marketing, documentation systems, and full-time developers who can maintain the code. These things cost money, and usually it is a company that pays for them. Sun Microsystems invested heavily in OpenOffice.org by releasing the code, managing the project, and supporting its development for many years. From this point of view, it may seem reasonable that Sun wanted to keep control over the project’s direction. If a company is responsible for funding most of the work, it may believe that it should also have the authority to make final decisions.
This argument becomes even stronger when we consider the risks involved. Companies that support open source projects may face legal challenges, competition, and financial losses. If a project fails, the company loses money, while volunteers only lose their time. Because of this, some people believe that corporate governance is necessary to keep large projects stable. Without a clear authority, decision-making can become slow and disorganized, especially when many contributors disagree. In the case of OpenOffice.org, Sun may have believed that centralized control was the only way to ensure that the project continued to grow and remain competitive with Microsoft Office.
However, there is also a strong argument against giving corporations too much control over open source projects. Open source software is often built by a community that believes in sharing knowledge freely. Volunteers contribute because they want to create something useful for everyone, not because they want a company to own their work. When contributors are required to sign copyright assignment agreements, it can feel like their effort is being taken and turned into corporate property. Even if the company originally created the project, the community may feel that the software belongs to everyone who helped build it.
This is why many developers were uncomfortable with the copyright assignment policy in OpenOffice.org. Under this system, contributors had to give joint ownership of their code to Sun, even though they were not employees of the company. While Sun argued that this was necessary for legal reasons, many volunteers believed that it gave the company too much power. The situation became worse after Oracle acquired Sun, because Oracle had a reputation for protecting its business interests very aggressively. The same policy that seemed acceptable under Sun began to look unfair under Oracle, showing that ethics in open source governance often depend on trust as much as on rules.
The copyright assignment clause in the OpenOffice.org project is one of the most controversial parts of the entire story. Sun required every contributor to sign an agreement that allowed the company to share ownership of the code they wrote. From a legal perspective, this made it easier for the company to manage the project, because it ensured that all parts of the software could be distributed under the same license. Sun argued that without copyright assignment, it would be difficult to protect the project from legal disputes or incompatible contributions. In this sense, the policy can be seen as practical rather than unethical.
However, the ethical question becomes more complicated when we consider the position of the volunteers. Contributors were not paid employees, yet their work became partially owned by a corporation. Many developers felt that this created an imbalance of power. The community provided creativity and effort, while the company held the legal rights. Under Sun, this arrangement was tolerated because Sun was generally trusted to act in the interest of the project. When Oracle took control, the same agreement suddenly felt dangerous. Contributors worried that the rights they had signed away could be used in ways that did not benefit the community at all.
This difference in reaction shows that the ethics of copyright assignment depend on who holds the power and how that power is used. If a company behaves like a steward, meaning it manages the project for the benefit of everyone, contributors may accept strong corporate authority. But if the company behaves like an owner, meaning it treats the project as its private property, the same authority becomes unacceptable. In the case of OpenOffice.org, many developers believed that Sun had acted more like a steward, while Oracle appeared to act more like an owner. Even if the legal structure did not change, the perception of control changed completely.
The idea of stewardship versus ownership is very important in open source projects. Ownership suggests that the company has the right to make decisions based on its own interests, even if the community disagrees. Stewardship suggests that the company has responsibility to protect the project for everyone who depends on it. In a healthy open source project, contributors usually expect the company to behave as a steward rather than as a strict owner. When this expectation is broken, trust disappears, and the community may decide that it cannot continue under the same leadership.
In my opinion, corporations do have the right to influence the projects they invest in, but that right should have limits. If a company wants full control, then the project should not depend on volunteer contributions. When a project is built by a community, the community should also have a voice in how it is governed. Sun’s copyright assignment rule may have been understandable, but it created a system where the balance of power was too heavily in favor of the company. When Oracle inherited that system, the lack of trust made the problem impossible to ignore. This shows that ethical governance in open source requires not only legal agreements but also fairness, transparency, and respect for the people who contribute their work.
Another important point in this debate is whether corporate investment automatically gives a company the moral right to control an open source project. Supporters of strong corporate governance argue that without companies, many large open source projects would not exist at all. Corporations provide funding, professional developers, infrastructure, and long-term stability. From this perspective, it may seem unfair for volunteers to demand equal authority when they are not responsible for the financial risks. If a company spends millions of dollars maintaining a project, it is understandable that it wants to protect its interests and ensure that the project follows a direction that supports its business goals.
On the other hand, open source software is different from normal commercial products because it depends on trust and cooperation. Volunteers contribute not because they are required to, but because they believe the project belongs to a shared community. When a company uses community contributions while keeping complete control over decisions, it can feel like the community’s effort is being used without giving them real influence. This creates a situation where contributors may feel that they are working for the company without being employees. In the long term, such a system is unstable, because people are less willing to contribute if they think their work will only increase corporate power.
The OpenOffice.org case shows that legal rights alone are not enough to maintain a healthy open source project. Sun had the legal right to require copyright assignment, and Oracle had the legal right to continue the same policy. However, the community’s reaction proves that ethical legitimacy is different from legal authority. Even though Oracle did nothing illegal, many contributors believed that the company did not respect the spirit of open source collaboration. Once that belief spread, the project lost the trust of its community, and no legal rule could prevent the fork from happening.
For this reason, I believe that corporations should act as stewards rather than owners when they control open source projects. Stewardship means that the company protects the project, supports its growth, and benefits from it, but does not treat it as private property. Ownership, in contrast, allows the company to make decisions without considering the community, which can lead to conflict and eventually to forks. Sun’s system already placed too much power in corporate hands, but the community accepted it because there was trust. When Oracle took control, the same structure became unacceptable, proving that trust is more important than legal agreements.
In conclusion, corporate investment does not give unlimited moral authority over an open source project. Companies deserve influence when they provide resources, but that influence should be balanced with community participation. The failure to maintain this balance in OpenOffice.org led directly to the creation of LibreOffice. The lesson from this case is that open source projects survive not only because of code and licenses, but because of fairness, transparency, and mutual respect between companies and contributors.

Section C — The Forking Question
The decision to fork OpenOffice.org and create LibreOffice was one of the most important moments in the history of open source software. Forking is always allowed in open source projects because the license gives anyone the freedom to copy and modify the code. However, the fact that something is legally possible does not mean it is always the best choice. Forking divides the community, creates confusion for users, and requires a large amount of effort to rebuild the project’s infrastructure. Because of these risks, the decision to fork should only be taken when there is no realistic alternative. In the case of OpenOffice.org, the community believed that the situation under Oracle had reached a point where continuing under the same governance was no longer acceptable.
One possible argument is that the community should have tried harder to work within Oracle’s system instead of creating a new project. OpenOffice.org already had millions of users, a well-known name, and an established development process. By forking, the community risked losing all of this momentum. It is possible that if the developers had stayed, they could have negotiated changes with Oracle over time. Large companies sometimes change their policies when they see that the community is unhappy, especially if the success of the project depends on volunteer contributions. From this perspective, forking may seem like a drastic reaction that created unnecessary division.
However, the actions of Oracle made many contributors believe that cooperation would not lead to real change. The company did not remove the copyright assignment requirement, did not give the community more authority, and did not communicate openly about the future of the project. Instead, Oracle treated OpenOffice.org as a product that it controlled. Because of this, the community felt that staying within the project would only make them more dependent on a company that did not share their values. Forking became a way to protect the long-term future of the software, even though it caused short-term problems.
Another important moment was when The Document Foundation invited Oracle to donate the OpenOffice.org brand and join the new governance structure. From the community’s point of view, this was a reasonable compromise. The foundation did not want to destroy the existing project; it wanted to create a system where no single company would have complete control. Oracle refused this invitation, which forced contributors to choose between the original project and the new fork. From a business perspective, Oracle’s decision can be understood. Giving up control of the brand would mean losing the ability to use the project for commercial purposes. For a company that had invested money in acquiring Sun Microsystems, keeping control may have seemed like the logical choice.
From an ethical perspective, however, the decision looks different. By refusing to cooperate, Oracle showed that it valued ownership more than community trust. This choice may have protected the company’s short-term interests, but it damaged the long-term health of the project. The result was that most contributors left, and the original project lost much of its activity. This shows that decisions that make sense in business terms are not always the best decisions for an open source community.
Forking OpenOffice.org did not only affect developers; it also created confusion for users and organizations that depended on the software. For several years, many people were unsure whether they should continue using OpenOffice.org or switch to LibreOffice. Both projects were based on the same code, both were free, and both claimed to be the continuation of the original idea. This situation caused hesitation, especially for governments and institutions that needed stability before adopting a software platform. Because of this, some critics argue that the fork created unnecessary fragmentation and slowed down progress that could have continued if the community had stayed united.
A strong argument against the fork is that the community might have achieved better results by remaining inside the original project and trying to influence Oracle from within. Large projects often require compromise, and it is possible that over time Oracle could have given more authority to the community if developers had continued contributing. Forking meant starting again with a new organization, new branding, and new infrastructure, which required a lot of effort that could otherwise have been used to improve the software itself. From this point of view, the fork can be seen as a risky decision that sacrificed stability for the sake of principle.
However, this argument becomes less convincing when we consider the importance of trust in open source development. A project can only succeed if contributors believe that their work will be respected and that the project will remain open in the future. In the case of OpenOffice.org, many developers no longer trusted Oracle to act as a fair steward. Without trust, even the best technical project cannot survive, because volunteers will stop contributing. If the community had stayed under Oracle’s control, the project might have continued for some time, but it would probably have lost more and more contributors until development slowed down anyway. In this sense, the fork may have been difficult, but it prevented a slow decline.
Looking at the results today, it seems that the decision to fork was worth the cost. LibreOffice became the main continuation of the project, with more developers, more frequent updates, and wider adoption. OpenOffice.org, which later moved to the Apache Software Foundation, continued to exist but with much less activity. This outcome suggests that the community’s decision was not only emotional but also practical. By creating a new foundation with shared governance, contributors ensured that the project could grow without depending on a single company.
In my opinion, the fork was the right response to the situation. Even though it created short-term confusion, it protected the independence of the project and restored trust between developers. The strongest argument against the fork is that unity is better than division, but unity only works when all sides respect each other. When one side has complete control and refuses to share it, division may be the only way to preserve the original values of the community. The LibreOffice case shows that the freedom to fork is not just a legal rule, but an essential protection that allows open source projects to survive when governance fails.

Section D — Lessons and Generalisation
The story of LibreOffice is not an isolated event. A very similar situation happened with the MySQL database around the same time when Oracle acquired Sun Microsystems. MySQL was one of the most widely used open source databases, and like OpenOffice.org, it was controlled by a company even though many people outside the company contributed to it. When Oracle became the owner of MySQL, the original creator of the database, Michael Widenius, feared that the project would no longer remain fully open. Because of this concern, he created a fork called MariaDB, which was designed to remain community-controlled and independent from corporate ownership. Today, MariaDB is widely used and has replaced MySQL in many Linux distributions.
The fact that two major projects forked after the same corporate acquisition shows a clear pattern. When a company gains ownership of an open source project without strong community governance, trust can quickly disappear. Even if the company does not immediately change the license or stop development, contributors may worry about the future. Open source projects depend on long-term cooperation, and that cooperation is only possible when developers believe that the project will not suddenly become controlled by commercial interests. The LibreOffice and MariaDB forks both demonstrate that open source communities are willing to take difficult steps to protect their independence when they feel that trust has been broken.
This pattern also helps us understand modern situations where large technology companies control popular open source projects. Imagine a company that contributes most of the code to a project, controls the decision-making process, and uses the project to support its own commercial services. In this situation, the project may still be open source legally, but the real power belongs to the company. This is very similar to what happened with OpenOffice.org. The important question is not only who writes the code, but who decides the future of the project. If the governance structure allows one organization to make all major decisions, the community may eventually feel that the project is not truly open.
To decide whether a corporate steward can be trusted, several criteria should be considered. First, the decision-making process should be transparent, so that contributors understand how choices are made. Second, no single company should have the power to control the project completely. Third, contributors should keep ownership of their work instead of being forced to give copyright to the company. Finally, communication between the company and the community should be open and respectful. If these conditions are not present, the project may remain technically open source, but it will not have the trust needed for long-term success.
The LibreOffice case teaches that governance is just as important as licensing. A project can follow all the rules of open source licensing and still fail if the community feels excluded from decision-making. For this reason, developers starting new projects today must think carefully about how the project will be managed, not only about how the code will be shared.
If I were advising a group of developers who are starting a new open source project today, I would recommend a governance structure that prevents any single company from gaining complete control over the project. One of the most important rules should be that contributors keep the copyright to their own work. Instead of requiring copyright assignment to a company, the project could use a contributor license agreement that allows the code to be used in the project without transferring ownership. This would make sure that no corporation can later claim full legal control over the entire codebase. When contributors know that their work remains theirs, they are more likely to trust the project and continue participating.
Another important rule would be to create an independent foundation to manage the project. The foundation should be non-profit and should have a governing board made up of different members from the community, including individual contributors as well as representatives from companies. To avoid the problems seen in OpenOffice.org, the rules should limit how much influence any single organization can have. For example, no company should be allowed to hold more than a certain percentage of seats on the board. This kind of system ensures that companies can support the project without being able to control it completely.
Decision-making should also be transparent and based on discussion rather than authority. Important choices such as release schedules, new features, or changes in license should be made through open meetings or public discussions where contributors can give their opinions. Even if the final decision is made by a smaller group, the process should be visible to everyone. Transparency helps maintain trust, because contributors can see that decisions are not being made secretly for the benefit of one organization.
It is also important to define the difference between corporate participation and corporate ownership. Companies should be encouraged to contribute developers, funding, and infrastructure, because large projects often need professional support. However, contributing resources should not automatically give a company the right to control the project. The goal of open source is cooperation, not domination by the strongest participant. A healthy project allows companies to benefit from the software while still protecting the independence of the community.
The main lesson from the LibreOffice and MariaDB forks is that governance problems may not appear immediately, but they can become serious when leadership changes. A project that seems stable under one company may become unstable under another. Because of this, good governance rules must be designed from the beginning, before a crisis happens. If OpenOffice.org had used a more balanced structure from the start, the fork that created LibreOffice might never have been necessary.

Section E — Reflection
Before reading this case study, I thought that open source software simply meant software that is free to use and whose code is available to everyone. I believed that as long as a program was open source, it automatically belonged to the community and could not be controlled by any single company. After learning about the story of OpenOffice.org and LibreOffice, my understanding has changed. I realized that open source does not only depend on the license of the code, but also on how the project is governed. A project can be open source legally, but still be controlled in practice by one organization. This difference between legal openness and practical openness was something I had never thought about before.
One thing that surprised me in this story was how much trust matters in open source communities. Under Sun Microsystems, many contributors accepted the copyright assignment rule even though it gave the company strong control. When Oracle took over, the same rule became unacceptable to many people. This shows that rules alone do not decide whether a project feels fair. The behavior and reputation of the people in control are also very important. I found it interesting that the fork did not happen because of a change in the license, but because contributors no longer trusted the company that owned the project.
I also felt that the copyright assignment policy was unfair to volunteers. When someone spends their free time writing code for an open source project, it seems wrong that a company can become the legal owner of that work. At the same time, I understand that companies need some control if they are investing money and resources. This makes the situation complicated, because both sides have reasonable arguments. The case study made me realize that open source projects must find a balance between corporate support and community independence, otherwise conflicts will eventually appear.
The decision of the LibreOffice community to fork the project shows how strongly they believed in their principles. By creating a new foundation, they gave up the advantage of the original project name and the existing infrastructure. This must have been a very difficult decision, because there was no guarantee that the new project would succeed. In the short term, the fork caused confusion for users and divided the community. However, in the long term, LibreOffice became more successful, which suggests that the decision was worth the risk.
Personally, I think I would have supported the fork if I were part of the community at that time. Even though staying with the original project might have been easier, working under a system that I did not trust would make it difficult to stay motivated. Open source depends on voluntary effort, and people will not continue contributing if they feel that their work is controlled by someone else. The LibreOffice story shows that freedom in open source is not only about the code, but also about the ability of the community to decide its own future.
